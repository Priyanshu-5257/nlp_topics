### 1. Word2Vec (CBOW & Skip-gram) (High-Frequency)

Word2Vec is a milestone framework developed by Google in 2013. It uses a shallow neural network to learn dense vector representations (embeddings) of words based on their local context. It has two main architectures:

- **CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words):** The model tries to predict a missing center word based on the surrounding context words.
    - _Analogy:_ It’s like a fill-in-the-blank test. Given `["The", "cat", "___", "on", "the"]`, it predicts `"sat"`. CBOW is generally faster to train and has slightly better accuracy for frequent words.
- **Skip-gram:** The exact inverse of CBOW. It takes a single center word and tries to predict the surrounding context words.
    - _Analogy:_ Given the word `"sat"`, it tries to output high probabilities for words like `"cat"`, `"on"`, `"mat"`. Skip-gram is slower but works much better for representing rare words.

### 2. GloVe (Global Vectors) (High-Frequency)

Developed by Stanford, GloVe approaches word embeddings from a different mathematical angle than Word2Vec.

- **How it works:** While Word2Vec only looks at local context windows (predicting a word based on a few adjacent words), GloVe leverages **global** statistical information. It constructs a massive word-context co-occurrence matrix for the entire corpus and applies matrix factorization.
- **The Math:** GloVe trains the word vectors such that the dot product of two vectors equals the logarithm of their probability of co-occurrence in the entire dataset. It combines the benefits of classical matrix factorization (like LSA) with the local context window benefits of Word2Vec.

### 3. FastText (subword) (High-Frequency)

Developed by Facebook, FastText is a direct, highly popular extension of the Word2Vec skip-gram model.

- **The Problem with Word2Vec/GloVe:** They treat words as atomic, indivisible units. They cannot handle Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) words (words they didn't see during training), nor do they understand that "run" and "running" share a morphological root.
- **How FastText works:** It represents each word as a bag of character n-grams (subwords). For example, the word "apple" (with n=3) might be represented by the subwords `<ap`, `app`, `ppl`, `ple`, `le>`. The final embedding for "apple" is the sum of these subword vectors.
- **Why it's powerful:** If FastText encounters a brand new word like "applesauce" during testing, it can dynamically construct a meaningful vector for it by summing the embeddings of its known subwords (`app`, `ple`, `sau`, etc.).

### 4. Negative Sampling (High-Frequency)

This is an incredibly important optimization trick used to train Word2Vec and FastText efficiently.

- **The Problem:** In standard neural network training, to update the weights for predicting the next word, you have to calculate the probability of _every_ word in your vocabulary (e.g., 100,000 words) using a massive softmax function, which is too computationally expensive.
- **How it works:** Instead of updating the weights for all 99,999 incorrect words, Negative Sampling updates the weights for the 1 true target word (the positive sample) and only a tiny handful (usually 5 to 20) of randomly chosen incorrect words (the negative samples). It turns a massive multi-class classification problem into a few binary logistic regression problems.

### 5. Noise Contrastive Estimation (NCE)

NCE is the theoretical predecessor to Negative Sampling.

- **How it works:** It is a statistical method that tries to differentiate data generated by the true underlying distribution (the actual text) from data generated by a known "noise" distribution. While Negative Sampling is a simplified approximation designed specifically for word embeddings, NCE is a more mathematically rigorous approach to estimating probability distributions without having to compute a full softmax denominator.

### 6. Analogy Tasks (king - man + woman)

This is the most famous evaluation method for static word embeddings. It demonstrates that the spatial geometry of the vectors successfully captures deep semantic and syntactic relationships.

- **How it works:** Because the embeddings are dense vectors in a continuous space, you can perform vector algebra on them. The vector for "King" minus the vector for "Man" leaves a vector representing royalty/rulership. If you add the vector for "Woman" to that, the resulting point in space lands closest to the embedding for "Queen".
- **Other Analogies:** It also works for syntax (e.g., `Paris - France + Italy = Rome`, or `walking - walk + swim = swimming`).
