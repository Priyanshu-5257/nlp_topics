
### 1. POS Tagging (High-Frequency)

Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging is the process of assigning a grammatical category (like noun, verb, adjective, adverb) to every word in a sequence.

- **How it works:** Because many words can act as multiple parts of speech depending on context (e.g., "I will _book_ a flight" vs. "I read a _book_"), models look at surrounding words to disambiguate. As covered in the Markov Models section, Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) are a classic algorithm for this task.
- **NLP Example:** The sentence `"The fast runner ran fast"` would be tagged as: `["The" (Determiner), "fast" (Adjective), "runner" (Noun), "ran" (Verb), "fast" (Adverb)]`.

### 2. Constituency Parsing

This type of parsing breaks a sentence down into a nested hierarchy of "constituents" or sub-phrases based on specific grammar rules.

- **How it works:** It uses phrase structure grammar to group words into larger syntactic units, like Noun Phrases (NP), Verb Phrases (VP), and Prepositional Phrases (PP).
- **NLP Example:** The sentence `"The cat sat on the mat"` would be parsed into a tree structure. The root is a Sentence (S), which splits into an NP (`"The cat"`) and a VP (`"sat on the mat"`). The VP further splits into a Verb (`"sat"`) and a PP (`"on the mat"`), and so on.

### 3. Dependency Parsing (High-Frequency)

Instead of grouping words into nested phrases, dependency parsing maps the direct, grammatical relationships _between individual words_.

- **How it works:** It connects a "head" word to a "dependent" word with a specific labeled relation (e.g., subject, object, modifier). The result is a directed graph (usually a tree) originating from a single "root" word (usually the main verb).
- **NLP Example:** In `"The cat sat"`, `"sat"` is the Root. `"cat"` is the nominal subject (nsubj) dependent on `"sat"`. `"The"` is the determiner (det) dependent on `"cat"`.
- **Why it's important:** It is highly favored in modern NLP because it effectively captures long-distance dependencies and handles languages with flexible word orders much better than constituency parsing.

### 4. CFG & CYK Algorithm

These are the formal mathematical tools used to execute constituency parsing.

- **CFG (Context-Free Grammar):** A set of recursive rules used to generate patterns of strings.
    - **Expression:** Rules look like $S \to NP \ VP$ (a Sentence is made of a Noun Phrase and a Verb Phrase), or $NP \to \text{Det} \ \text{Noun}$ (a Noun Phrase is made of a Determiner and a Noun).
- **CYK Algorithm (Cocke-Younger-Kasami):** A bottom-up, dynamic programming algorithm used to determine if a string can be generated by a specific CFG, and if so, how to parse it. It requires the CFG to be converted into a strict format called Chomsky Normal Form (CNF).
    - **Complexity:** It operates in $O(n^3)$ time complexity, where $n$ is the length of the sentence, which makes it computationally heavy for long texts.

### 5. Named Entity Recognition (NER) (High-Frequency)

NER is an information extraction task that seeks to locate and classify named entities mentioned in unstructured text into pre-defined categories such as person names, organizations, locations, medical codes, time expressions, quantities, monetary values, etc.

- **How it works:** It is typically framed as a "sequence labeling" task (often using Conditional Random Fields or standard Neural Networks). Models classify each token to determine if it is the beginning, inside, or outside of an entity.
- **NLP Example:** `"Tim Cook is the CEO of Apple Inc. in Cupertino."`
    - `"Tim Cook"` $\to$ **Person**
    - `"Apple Inc."` $\to$ **Organization**
    - `"Cupertino"` $\to$ **Location**

### 6. Chunking / Shallow Parsing

Chunking is a simplified, faster version of constituency parsing. Instead of building a full, nested syntactic tree of the whole sentence, it simply groups adjacent words into non-overlapping grammatical "chunks."

- **How it works:** It usually just identifies the base phrases, such as Noun Phrases (NP) or Verb Phrases (VP), without caring about how they interact with each other in the larger sentence structure.
- **NLP Example:** In the sentence `"The bright red car is parked on the street"`, a chunker would output `[NP: The bright red car]`, `[VP: is parked]`, and `[NP: the street]`, ignoring the hierarchical structure that connects them.
